What Is a Hypothesis?

A hypothesis is a tentative, testable statement that predicts the relationship between two or more variables. It translates your research question into a specific, falsifiable prediction that can be evaluated through data collection and analysis.

Key Principle
A good hypothesis is specific, testable, falsifiable, and grounded in existing theory or observation. If you cannot imagine data that would disprove your statement, it is not a hypothesis.

The Five Pillars of a Strong Hypothesis

GroundedRooted in literature, theory, or observationCan I cite prior work supporting this?
ParsimoniousAs simple as the phenomenon allowsAm I testing one relationship, not five?

Types: Hypothesis vs Proposition vs Guiding Hypothesis

Type Definition When to Use Testability
Hypothesis A testable, falsifiable prediction about the relationship between measurable (observable) variables. Quantitative research, experiments, surveys with established scales. Directly testable via statistics (t-test, regression, ANOVA, etc.)
Proposition A statement about the relationship between constructs (abstract, theoretical concepts) — often untestable until operationalised. Theory-building research, conceptual frameworks, when variables are latent. Tested indirectly — must be broken into measurable hypotheses first.
Guiding Hypothesis A broad, directional assumption that orients an exploratory study without making a strict prediction. Qualitative research, grounded-theory studies, early-stage exploratory work. Not statistically tested — assessed through thematic analysis, pattern-matching, etc.

Research Topic: Impact of leadership style on employee engagement

Proposition: "Transformational leadership enhances organisational commitment."
↑ Both concepts are abstract constructs — not directly measurable yet.
Hypothesis: "Employees who score their managers ≥ 4.0 on the MLQ-5X transformational leadership scale will report significantly higher scores on the OCQ affective commitment subscale than employees whose managers score < 3.0."
↑ Variables are operationalised with specific instruments and cut-offs.
Guiding Hypothesis: "Leadership practices in the organisation may be shaping how employees relate to their work."
↑ Broad orientation for an interview-based qualitative study.

Declarative vs If-Then Hypothesis Format

Declarative (Statement) Form

Directly states the expected relationship. Preferred in most social science, management, and health research journals.

H1: "There is a significant positive relationship between employee training hours and job performance ratings."

IV: Training hours DV: Job performance ratings

Test: Pearson correlation or simple linear regression. Significance at p < 0.05 supports H1.

H2: "Students who use spaced repetition will score higher on the final exam than students who use massed practice."

IV: Study method (spaced vs massed) DV: Final exam score

Test: Independent samples t-test comparing mean exam scores between groups.

If-Then (Conditional) Form

Structures the hypothesis as a conditional prediction. Common in experimental and natural science research.

H3: "If classroom temperature increases above 28°C, then students' sustained attention scores will decrease."

IV: Classroom temperature DV: Sustained attention score

Test: Manipulate temperature across conditions; compare attention scores via repeated-measures ANOVA.

H4: "If a customer receives a personalised email, then purchase likelihood will increase, and this effect will be stronger for returning customers than new customers."

IV: Email type DV: Purchase likelihood Moderator: Customer type

Test: 2×2 factorial ANOVA (email type × customer type), examining the interaction effect.

05

And / Or Hypotheses

Compound hypotheses use "and" or "or" to combine multiple predictions into a single statement. This is usually a bad idea because it creates ambiguity about what exactly is being tested and makes falsification messy.

The "And" Hypothesis Problem

H: "Social media usage increases anxiety AND decreases academic performance."

Why it fails: This is actually two hypotheses bundled into one. What if social media usage increases anxiety but has no effect on academic performance? The hypothesis is partially supported — which makes your results section confusing and your statistical testing ambiguous.

H1a: "Social media usage is positively associated with anxiety levels."
H1b: "Social media usage is negatively associated with academic performance."

Now each can be tested, reported, and supported or rejected independently.

The "Or" Hypothesis Problem

H: "Remote work increases productivity OR job satisfaction."

Why it fails: An "or" hypothesis is almost impossible to falsify — it is supported if either outcome occurs. This means there is practically no result that would count as evidence against it.

H2a: "Remote work is positively associated with employee productivity."
H2b: "Remote work is positively associated with job satisfaction."
Rule of Thumb
One hypothesis = one relationship. If you see "and" or "or" in your hypothesis, you almost certainly need to split it.

Common Hypothesis Mistakes & Pitfalls

Mistake Bad Example Why It's Wrong Fixed Version
Too vague "Technology affects learning." Which technology? Which aspect of learning? Affects how? "Tablet-based instruction improves Grade 5 students' reading comprehension scores."
Not falsifiable "Communication is important in relationships." No possible evidence could disprove this truism. "Couples who engage in ≥ 30 min of daily quality conversation report higher relationship satisfaction on the RAS."
No direction
(when one is needed)		 "There is a relationship between diet and mood." Doesn't predict what kind of relationship — positive? Negative? "A Mediterranean diet is positively associated with self-reported mood scores."
Compound / "and-or" "Exercise reduces stress and improves sleep." Two claims in one — partial support is ambiguous. Split into H1a (stress) and H1b (sleep).
Confuses IV and DV "Higher grades cause students to study more." Reverse causation — grades are typically the outcome. "Increased study hours lead to higher exam grades."
Value-laden "Organic food is better than processed food." "Better" is subjective and not measurable. "Consumers of organic diets exhibit lower BMI than consumers of processed-food-dominant diets."
Tautological "Happy people experience more happiness." The prediction is the definition — circular reasoning. "Employees with high gratitude scores report greater life satisfaction."

Non-Measurable Hypotheses & How to Fix Them

A hypothesis is non-measurable when one or more of its variables cannot be quantified, observed, or captured with any known instrument. This is the single most common reason committee members reject hypotheses.

"Spiritual leadership improves the soul of the organisation."

Problem: "Soul of the organisation" has no operational definition, no scale, and no agreed-upon measurement. No data could confirm or deny this.

"Spiritual leadership (measured via the Fry Spiritual Leadership Scale) is positively associated with organisational commitment (measured via the Allen-Meyer OCQ)."

Both variables now have validated instruments. The relationship can be tested with correlation or regression.

How to Test Propositions

Because propositions deal with abstract constructs, they cannot be tested directly. Instead, you follow a four-stage process:

Proposition: "Organisational justice influences employee voice behaviour."

Step 1 — Operationalise:

Organisational Justice → Colquitt's (2001) Justice Scale (4 dimensions: distributive, procedural, interpersonal, informational)
Employee Voice → Van Dyne & LePine's (1998) Voice Behaviour Scale (6 items)

Step 2 — Derive Hypotheses:

H1: Procedural justice is positively related to voice behaviour.
H2: Distributive justice is positively related to voice behaviour.
H3: Interpersonal justice is positively related to voice behaviour.
H4: Informational justice is positively related to voice behaviour.

Step 3 — Test: Hierarchical multiple regression or SEM (structural equation modelling). Each path coefficient tests one hypothesis.

Step 4 — Infer Back: If H1, H2, H3, and H4 are supported, the original proposition receives empirical support. If only some are supported, the proposition is partially supported — and you refine the theory.

Key Insight
Propositions live at the theory level. Hypotheses live at the data level. Your research moves between these two levels constantly. SEM is especially powerful for this because it models latent constructs directly.

Latent Variables Explained

A latent variable is a construct that cannot be directly observed or measured with a single question. It is inferred from multiple observed indicators (items on a questionnaire, behavioural measures, etc.).

Observable (Manifest) Variable

Directly measurable: age, salary, temperature, test score, number of absences.

Appears as a rectangle in SEM path diagrams.

Latent (Unobserved) Variable

Inferred from indicators: intelligence, motivation, job satisfaction, brand loyalty.

Appears as an ellipse / circle in SEM path diagrams.

Why Latent Variables Matter for Hypotheses

When your hypothesis includes a latent variable, you must explain how you will measure it. This means specifying the observed indicators that reflect the underlying construct. Without this, the hypothesis is non-testable.

Latent Construct: Emotional Intelligence (EI)

Observed Indicators (items): Self-awareness subscale score, self-regulation subscale score, empathy subscale score, social skills subscale score — all from the Wong & Law EI Scale (WLEIS).

H: "Emotional intelligence (measured via WLEIS) positively predicts conflict resolution effectiveness (measured via the ROCI-II integrating style score) among middle managers."

Latent IV: EI DV: Conflict resolution score

Test: SEM with EI as a latent factor (4 indicators) predicting the observed DV, or CFA + path analysis.

Common Latent Variables in Research

Motivation, self-efficacy, organisational culture, brand equity, quality of life, customer satisfaction, perceived risk, trust, engagement, burnout, psychological capital — all of these require multi-item measurement and cannot be captured with a single question.

Independent & Dependent Variable Selection

Independent Variable (IV)

The presumed cause or predictor. It is what you manipulate (in experiments) or measure first (in surveys). It is the variable you believe influences the outcome.

Ask: "What am I changing or examining as the input?"

Dependent Variable (DV)

The presumed effect or outcome. It is what you measure to see if the IV had an impact. It "depends on" the IV.

Ask: "What am I measuring as the result?"

Step-by-Step Variable Selection Process

StepActionExample
1. Start from theory Identify the causal model in your literature review. What does theory say causes what? Self-Determination Theory says autonomy support → intrinsic motivation.
2. Identify the direction Determine which variable logically comes first (temporally or causally). Autonomy support (IV) happens first; motivation (DV) is the consequence.
3. Operationalise Find validated instruments for each variable. IV: Work Climate Questionnaire (WCQ). DV: Intrinsic Motivation Inventory (IMI).
4. Check for confounders Identify control variables or mediators/moderators. Control for: tenure, age, role level. Possible mediator: perceived competence.
5. Write the hypothesis Combine IV, DV, direction, and any moderators/mediators into a clear statement. "Manager autonomy support (WCQ) positively predicts intrinsic motivation (IMI), mediated by perceived competence."

Variable Roles Beyond IV & DV

RoleDefinitionExample
Mediator M Explains why or how the IV affects the DV (the mechanism). Training (IV) → Self-efficacy (M) → Performance (DV)
Moderator Mod Changes the strength or direction of the IV → DV relationship. Feedback (IV) → Performance (DV), moderated by personality type.
Control Variable Held constant or statistically accounted for to rule out alternative explanations. Age, gender, tenure, education level.
Common IV/DV Mistakes
Reversing the direction: Writing "High grades lead to more studying" when the theory says studying → grades.
Picking DVs you can't measure: "Happiness" without a scale. Use the Subjective Happiness Scale (SHS) or SWLS instead.
Ignoring confounders: Not controlling for obvious third variables that could explain the relationship (e.g., income when studying education → health).
11

Master Checklist: Before You Finalise

  • My hypothesis states a clear relationship between exactly two variables (or one IV and one DV with stated mediator/moderator).
  • Both variables are operationally defined with specific instruments or measures named.
  • The direction of the relationship is stated (positive, negative, greater than, etc.) — unless I have justified a non-directional hypothesis.
  • The hypothesis is falsifiable — I can describe what data would disprove it.
  • The hypothesis is grounded in theory — I can cite at least one source supporting the predicted relationship.
  • There is no "and" or "or" joining two separate predictions. Each hypothesis tests one relationship.
  • I have correctly identified which is the IV and which is the DV based on temporal/causal logic.
  • If my variables are latent constructs, I have specified the indicators and measurement model.
  • I have identified control variables to rule out alternative explanations.
  • I know which statistical test will be used to evaluate the hypothesis.
  • My hypothesis avoids value judgments ("better", "worse", "should") and uses measurable terms.
  • I have checked that my population/sample is stated or clearly implied.
